Everything You Need to Know About Fishing

People have been fishing for years, with varying degrees of success. It seems a simple activity on the surface, after all, you just throw a hook and line into the water and voila, there¿s your lunch, right? Wrong! Fishing is a complex activity with many nuances and tricks of the trade. With 31,500 different species of fish in the world, the variety of fishing techniques and equipment to catch them with is mind blowing. So, what are the basics of fishing that you need to check out? Firstly, you need to decide what type of fishing you will be doing. Fresh water fishing, ocean fishing, fishing for sport or simply fishing for leisure all involve different techniques and equipment, so try to narrow down your interests. Once you¿ve decided on the type of fishing that interests you most, do your research. The Internet is full of hints and tips for just about all categories of fishing and a simple search for fishing techniques can return literally millions of sources of useful information. A great place to find out more about fishing is at your local tackle shop. Not only are the staff there knowledgeable about the equipment they sell, but they are usually enthusiastic fishermen or women who can pass on many great tips.

Many people don’t like the idea of a ‘hike’. Immediately they associate the word with being slumped under a heavy pack, plodding up a slope in bulky, blister-causing boots with nothing to look forward to except warm water from a smelly bottle. Forget that, here’s a cool selection of walks and trails to suit all levels of fitness and experience - go get out there. ROYAL NATAL NATIONAL PARK, KWAZULU-NATAL Many of the Drakensberg’s finest treasures are accessible only to those who know how to cook on a small propane stove and live on instant noodles for days. Fortunately, there are many places in the dragon’s lair you can enjoy without becoming a full-on mountain goat. For the best day hikes, base yourself near the Royal Natal National Park in the northern Drakensberg. Most trails are well mapped and clearly marked and range from hour-long strolls to McKinley’s Pool and the Cascades, to more strenuous treks to the Mahai Falls. Stay for a few days and ease into the mountain rhythms with these shorter walks, then graduate to the Thukela Gorge. It’s a 14-kilometre round trip through mountain forest and protea grassland that will take you from Tendele Camp along the Thukela River and deep into the fold of the imposing Amphitheatre (but not to the top). HOERIKWAGGO TRAILS, WESTERN CAPE Table Mountain – of course you could simply hike up (via the popular Platteklip Gorge route) and ride the Cable Car down, but really, that’s for the tourists. The only way to really get in touch with, and understand the true significance of our most iconic natural symbol, is to overnight on it. The Hoerikwaggo Trail (the original Khoisan term for the mountain, meaning “Mountain in the Sea”) is a 75-kilometre traverse that will take five days. Because that’s beyond the skill (and time) level of most, it’s been broken into shorter, more manageable sections. We recommend the Silvermine Trail. From the Slangkop Lighthouse in Kommetjie, it’ll take you across the sands and vleis of Noordhoek and up Chapman’s Peak to Silvermine, where you’ll spend the night in a secluded tented camp. Swim in the dam instead of showering. The next day, traverse Constantiaberg to Constantia Nek and the end of the trail.
